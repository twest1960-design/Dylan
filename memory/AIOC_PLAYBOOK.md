# AIOC Playbook — Deep Dive

## Purpose
Comprehensive analysis of the Alberta Indigenous Opportunities Corporation (AIOC) to support preparation for discussions, loan guarantee applications, and deal structuring.

---

## What AIOC Is

**AIOC** is an independent Provincial Crown corporation created by the Government of Alberta in 2019. It manages a **$3 billion** Indigenous loan guarantee program designed to help Indigenous Nations and groups access capital for mid-to large-scale projects.

**Key point:** AIOC provides **loan guarantees**, not direct loans. It acts as a credit enhancement body that reduces lender risk, enabling Indigenous groups to access capital at favorable terms.

### Mandate
To facilitate investment by Indigenous groups in:
- Natural resources
- Agriculture
- Telecommunications
- Transportation
- Tourism
- Healthcare
- Technology
- Related infrastructure

---

## How AIOC Works

### The Core Mechanism
AIOC provides a **government-backed loan guarantee** that acts as collateral for lenders. If the borrower defaults, AIOC repays the lender and assumes ownership of the asset, then sells it to recover costs. Any shortfall is covered by the Government of Alberta's balance sheet.

**Key implication for Indigenous groups:**
- No community assets are at risk in the event of default
- Loan repayment comes from revenues generated by the asset itself
- Indigenous groups can access capital without putting community assets on the line

### The Financing Structure
1. Indigenous group identifies an investment opportunity
2. Bank/lender requires collateral or guarantee before providing equity loan
3. AIOC provides the guarantee, reducing lender risk
4. Indigenous group gains access to capital with improved lending terms

## Eligibility Requirements

### Who Can Apply
- Eligible Indigenous Nations and groups (First Nations, Métis communities)
- Nation-owned or community-owned business entities administered by an elected Council

**Important:** Economic development corporations **cannot apply directly** for loan guarantees, but Indigenous Nations/groups with approved guarantees can hire them as advisors.

### Project Must Meet
- Indigenous investment between $20M–$250M
- Falls within mandate sectors (including agriculture)
- Demonstrates benefit back to Alberta
- High probability of meaningful cashflow to Indigenous investors
- Commercially viable — applicant must be able to service the debt

---

## Selection Criteria (What AIOC Looks For)

AIOC evaluates projects on a **case-by-case basis**. Key criteria:

1. **Commercial Viability**
   - Project must generate revenue and service debt
   - Strong cash flow projections
   - Realistic financial assumptions

2. **Indigenous Economic Benefit**
   - Clear economic benefits to Indigenous partners
   - Meaningful ownership and governance participation
   - Revenue generation for Indigenous communities

3. **Operational Partner Quality**
   - Industry/investment partners must have significant skills and expertise
   - High-quality operators in their field
   - Proven track record

4. **Execution Credibility**
   - Clear permitting pathway
   - Realistic timelines
   - Experienced management team

## The Process — Phase by Phase

The typical deal process takes **six months to a year**:

### Phase 1: Opportunity Screening
- Indigenous group determines if they want to participate
- Key questions and considerations are worked through

### Phase 2: Introduction
- Industry partner facilitates formalizing intent
- May involve letters of intent and confidentiality agreements

### Phase 3: Project Kick-Off
- Formal launch of the process
- Typically involves non-binding LOI
- Negotiation committee is formed

### Phase 4: Formalize Negotiation Committee
- Advisory selection
- Roles and responsibilities outlined

### Phase 5: Due Diligence
- Detailed assessment including:
  - Project stage
  - Operational history (for existing assets)
  - Market assessment
  - Technology evaluation
  - Cash flow analysis/projections
- **AIOC conducts due diligence in parallel** if Indigenous group indicates likelihood of seeking guarantee

**Important:** Indigenous groups can apply for a **Capacity Grant** during this phase to fund legal, financial, and tax advisors

### Phase 6: Negotiations
- Deal terms negotiated (price, ownership, commercial terms)
- Legal contracts formalized

### Phase 7: Financing
- Indigenous group applies for AIOC loan guarantee
- Guarantee provided to financial institution

### Phase 8: Closing
- Legal documents signed
- Financing completed

### Phase 9: Active Partnership
- Ongoing financial and regulatory reporting
- Revenue flows to Indigenous partners per agreement

## Capacity Grants

**Purpose:** Help Indigenous Nations/groups access professional support for due diligence

**Key facts:**
- Up to **$3 million/year** awarded on discretionary basis
- Must be tied to probability of success for a loan guarantee
- Used for independent third-party legal, technical, economic advice
- **Does NOT need to be repaid**
- **Cannot be used for business plan development**

## What Works Well

Based on AIOC's criteria and Tom's experience:

- **Revenue-first positioning** — existing or near-term revenue is critical
- **Acquisition over greenfield** — buying operating assets is stronger than building from scratch
- **Clear, grounded business plans** — professional, structured, credible
- **Realistic financial projections** — conservative, supportable numbers
- **Strong Indigenous partnerships** — Métis Nation District 20 and others must have credible role
- **Phased development strategies** — demonstrate execution path
- **Lender alignment** — bank comfort is essential; AIOC will not carry deal without it
- **Transparent risk identification** — show you understand the challenges

---

## What Does NOT Work

- Purely conceptual projects without revenue
- Overly optimistic projections
- Unclear ownership structures
- Weak execution plans
- Lack of lender alignment
- Insufficient documentation

**Bottom line:** AIOC responds to structure and credibility, not enthusiasm.
